SPM Overview 9:15 — 20 min

📊 RPM Overview FY26 — Slides 19-20 (SPM Overview)

So let's start with some context. Sales Performance Management — SPM, or as Anaplan calls it, Revenue Performance Management — covers how you plan and manage your sales team. Territory design, quota setting, how many reps you need, how you track forecast.

There's no SPM degree. But companies that are mature at this have way more predictable revenue than those who aren't. And most companies are figuring it out in spreadsheets.

📊 RPM Overview FY26 — Slides 2-7 (T&Q overview, Territory Management, Quota Management, Challenges, Key Elements)

Anaplan has five SPM apps. Territory and Quota is the cornerstone — it's the most mature, most deployed, and honestly the one you'll land most often. The others — Segmentation, Capacity, Sales Forecasting, and eventually ICM — all connect to it.

T&Q is interesting because unlike ICM, where companies are basically stuck without enterprise tooling, T&Q is something companies manage with spreadsheets for a surprisingly long time. That means there's a lot of untapped demand. And when you land it, there's a clear expansion path to the other apps.

Go to slide 6 — "T&Q Key Elements." Point out the two columns: Territories (where to sell) and Quotas (how much to sell). These are distinct concepts in the app and it matters.

One thing I always call out early: in Anaplan, territories and quotas are separate objects. A target is set at the territory level — it's relatively stable. A quota is what an individual rep is measured against — it changes when people move in and out. This distinction trips people up.

Anaplan Way for Apps 9:35 — 20 min

📊 RPM Overview FY26 — Slide 8 (High-Level Anaplan Way for Applications)

Before we get into the configurator, let me walk you through how a T&Q implementation actually runs. This is the updated Anaplan Way — specifically for apps.

The big change from traditional Anaplan implementations: we split the foundation phase. You configure first, generate the app with the customer's data in it, and THEN do your extension design. The reason is simple — you can anchor every extension conversation on what's already there. About a third to a half of things customers think they need as extensions? They can actually do with the base app.

📊 RPM Overview FY26 — Slides 9-11 (Discovery Workshop)

Data readiness is the number one thing that kills project timelines. I can't stress this enough. Don't kick off a project until the customer has provided sample data and you've reviewed it. Once the clock starts, hours burn.

Share the Data Prep Workbook with them before the project starts — ideally before you've even agreed on a start date. If they come back with blanks in the mandatory fields, that's a red flag.

📝 This is a good moment to ask the room: "Has anyone had a project go sideways because of data? What happened?" Gets people engaged and surfaces real stories.

There are two types of customers you'll encounter. Type one wants it their way — always their way — and will push for heavy customization. Type two is maturing their planning process and wants you to tell them the right way to do it. Type two is your best partner. And honestly, if you always give type one exactly what they want, you'll burn out fast.

Configurator Walkthrough 10:15 — 60 min

📊 TnQ v2 Configurator Exercise 2026 — Slide 1 (Title)

Alright, let's get into the configurator. This is the heart of what makes apps different from custom builds. Instead of building everything from scratch, you fill out this configurator — think of it as a really smart form — and Anaplan generates a full T&Q model tailored to the customer.

Open up your configurator app in Anaplan. You should all have access. Search for "V2" or "V2 exercise" in your workspace.

📝 Give people 2 minutes to find it. Walk around (or scan the Zoom tiles) and help anyone who's stuck. Don't move on until everyone's in.
Navigate to Category 1 — Application Configurator. Categories 2 and 3 are back-end — participants don't need to touch those.

Category 1 is everything you'll work with. Categories 2 and 3 are mine to worry about when we actually generate.

Page 1 — Getting Started

Click Getting Started. Show the three checkboxes (T&Q, Data Hub, Spoke model). Show the missing inputs counter on the right.

Pre-populated — don't change these. The counter on the right tells you how many required inputs are still missing. Before we generate, that needs to be zero.

Page 2 — Data Hub Setup

Click Data Hub Setup.

This is the most important page in the entire configurator. Everything downstream flows from what you set up here.

These are your data objects — accounts, employees, territories, opportunities, orders. For each one, you define the attributes. Think of it like telling Anaplan what your data looks like.

Three booleans for each attribute — and these matter:

Hover over the Enable for Calculate, Auto-Create, and Enable for Validating checkboxes on any row.

Enable for Calculate: Anaplan pre-builds a list-formatted version of that attribute for you. Saves you legwork downstream. Use it freely.

Auto-Create: If a new value shows up in your data — say a new account rank — it automatically gets added to the list. Also use it freely.

Enable for Validating: Flags records with blank values in that field. Use sparingly — keep it under five attributes, or things get weird post-generation.

And here's the rule: after every page, hit Apply or Calculate before you move to the next one. The configurator is designed to be filled out in sequence — each page reflects what you've done so far.

📝 Emphasize the Apply button multiple times. It's the most common mistake in the exercise.

Page 3 — Hierarchy Setup

Click Hierarchy Setup.

This is where you define the levels of your territory hierarchy and what to call them. Whatever you name them here cascades through the entire app — UX labels, module names, everything. Get the naming right before you generate.

Page 5 — Planning Measures

Click Planning Measures (or Scenario Planning depending on your version).

Planning measures are your target types — Bookings, New ARR, Renewals, whatever the customer tracks. These become a core dimension through the entire target cascading process.

Here's the one I always warn people about: do not add a product dimension unless the customer absolutely has to have it. If they only set targets for three specific products, make those three planning measures — not a product dimension. A product dimension explodes the complexity of every page the planner touches.

📝 This gets questions. Common one: "What about sparsity in Polaris?" Answer: sparsity isn't a Polaris performance issue, but it's still a UX nightmare. Planners don't want to scroll through 1,000 products.

Page 9 — Target & Allocation

Navigate to Target & Allocation.

Three-step process. Step one: Finance drops in annual or quarterly targets at a defined hierarchy level. Step two: those targets cascade down using an allocation method — you pick the metric, like prior year actuals or potential spend. Step three: over-allocation and overrides.

Over-allocation is a percentage uplift at each level — say 5% — to account for attrition and ramp. Override is a hard number that supersedes everything. In V3, managers can override at every level. In V2, only one configured level.

Page 14 — Rule Engine

Navigate to Rule Engine Setup.

The rule engine is how you automatically assign accounts to territories based on attributes. Two buckets: geographic attributes — country, state, city — and firmographic attributes — revenue band, segment, industry. Key thing: these must be list-formatted. You can't use a raw number like employee count — it has to be a band like 0-250, 250-1000.

Completion & Generation

I'll review Categories 2 and 3, run it through the generation platform, and get you a generated app. It takes about 90 minutes to two hours and produces around 8,000 objects. Post-Generation Steps 1:00 — 30 min

📊 DRAFT - T&Q Planning App Post-Generation Updates V2.0 — first few slides

After generation, there's a checklist of post-gen activities before the app is ready to show a customer. It's about 52 pages, but most steps are 5 to 15 minutes. Total time: a day or two of model builder effort.

The most important thing to know: there's a known bug where the source model disappears the first time you run an import action. The fix is built into our migration process — when I move the generated app to the customer's workspace, that migration resolves it. So you won't hit it.

My recommendation: don't run the big "load everything" action the configurator generates. Parse it out into per-object actions — one for accounts, one for employees, one for opportunities. Way easier to troubleshoot.

Data Integration & ADO 1:30 — 30 min

📊 ADO Overview — first slides

Quick but important section. V3 apps — launching in May — drop the Data Hub entirely and move to ADO, Anaplan Data Orchestrator.

The key thing: ADO is inbound-only right now. It pulls from your sources, transforms data, and pushes into Anaplan on a schedule. It can't write back to Salesforce or Snowflake yet — that's coming.

If a customer already has a working Hub-Spoke setup, don't touch it. ADO is the path for new implementations and V3 upgrades, not a mid-project migration.

Show ADO Overview slides 1-3. Keep it brief — this isn't the main focus today.

One structural change for V3: territory data. In V2, one flat file with all tiers in columns. In V3, a separate ADO source view per tier. Five-tier hierarchy means five views. Not complicated, just different — flag it when you're doing data prep conversations.

Spoke App Walkthrough 2:00 — 90 min

Open the T&Q Spoke template app in Anaplan. Share your screen.

Now we walk through what the customer actually uses. This is the generated spoke app — what lands in their workspace at the end of model readiness.

Dashboards

Click the Overview/Dashboard category.

These dashboards are starting points. Don't show a customer these and say "here's your dashboard." Use them to start the conversation: what do you actually want to see here?

Account Information & Potential Spend

Navigate to Account Information → Potential Spend.

Potential Spend is the default allocation method — targets cascade proportionally based on this. If you're also implementing Segmentation, this is where those segment outputs land. Great talking point for selling the full suite.

Scenario Planning & Optimizer

Navigate to Scenario Planning → Scenario Comparison.

This is one of the coolest pages. Two scenarios side by side — as you move accounts between territories in scenario B, the delta highlights automatically. And after running the optimizer, you import results into a scenario here and compare before committing.

Navigate to Optimizer Setup.

The optimizer is one of the most powerful things in this app — and one of the least used in the industry because it's hard to set up from scratch. We give it to customers out of the box.

It's a balancing algorithm. Ranks accounts by your objective metric, distributes them round-robin across territories, respects your constraints. The result is roughly equal territories — not revenue-maximized, but balanced. And that's usually what customers need.

📝 Common question: "Can it exclude specific territories from being optimized?" Answer: not yet out of the box — known gap, likely coming.

Target Setup

Navigate to Target Setup → Finance Import.

Three steps. Finance enters targets here at the top. Step two, allocation method cascades them down. Step three, over-allocation and overrides.

One important thing: once a target level is approved, it locks. DCA kicks in. Make sure customers understand the approval sequence before they build their workflow.

Account Assignment — 4 Methods

Navigate to Account Assignment.

Four ways to assign accounts to territories. Manual — drag and drop essentially. Geo mapping lasso — draw a shape on a map, select the accounts inside, assign them. Needs lat/long on account records. Rule engine — define attribute-based rules per territory, system suggests which accounts match, you review and execute. And the optimizer, which we already covered.

Quickly show the Geo Mapping widget. Draw a lasso if the data is there.

Always test the geo mapping before demoing. Clear the lasso memory first — it can hold on to previous selections and behave weirdly.

Segmentation Configurator 9:10 — 45 min

Open the Configurator app. Navigate to the Segmentation section (scroll past the T&Q section in the nav).

Same configurator app, different section. Segmentation configurator is simpler than T&Q — maybe 40% of the effort. But the same principle applies: dimensionality decisions here are painful to change later.

The core question you're answering on every page: at what level of detail do you want to manage this assumption?

📊 Segmentation Planning Application Functional Overview — Slide 7 (Anaplan Segmentation App)

Quick overview of what the app does. Every account gets a segment — Strategic, Named, Commercial, SMB, whatever the customer uses. That segment then flows into T&Q for territory carving, and into Capacity for headcount planning. So getting segmentation right upstream saves a lot of pain downstream.

Data Setup

Mirror of the T&Q data setup. Same objects. Key addition: firmographic attributes. Employee count, revenue bands, industry. These need to be list-formatted — banded — because the rule engine can't work with raw numbers. Employee count of 503 doesn't work. Employee band of 250-1000 does.

Rule-Based Segmentation Setup

Show the Rule-Based Segmentation section in the configurator.

Define which attributes are available as rule dimensions. Configure your segment list. Fewer segments is almost always better — customers want ten, push them toward four or five. More segments means more complexity in every downstream app.

Score-Based Segmentation Setup

Up to ten scoring metrics, weights must sum to 100%. Score maps to a segment. Score-based gives every account a segment — even ones that don't hit your rules. Rule-based then acts as an override. Use both.

Wallet Size

Three methods: OpEx, Employee, or Revenue-based. Optional — if the customer won't use wallet size for allocation or segmentation rules, skip it. Don't over-engineer it.

📝 Segmentation configurator typically takes 2-3 hours with a prepared customer. T&Q is a week. Capacity is about 2 days.

Capacity Configurator 9:55 — 45 min

Scroll to the Capacity section of the same configurator.

Capacity is about answering one question: how many people do I need to hire, when, and where, to hit my revenue target?

Same dimensionality warning as T&Q. If you configure cost assumptions at the region level and later need sub-region, you're reconnecting a lot of formulas or regenerating. Be thorough.

📊 Go-to-Market Capacity Planning Functional Overview v1.1 — Slide 7 (Anaplan GTM Capacity App)

Role Hierarchy

Job Family Group → Job Family → Sales Role. Three levels. Role grade is optional and adds significant complexity — push back on it unless they genuinely need grade-level headcount planning.

Coverage & Workload

For direct sellers: account-based ratios. One Strategic AE per five accounts. For overlay and support: derived ratios. One manager per ten AEs. Hours-based workload planning is available but rarely used — revenue-based is the standard.

Revenue Target

At what level does Finance provide targets? And here's a known gotcha: if you configure revenue targets by account segment, the dimensionality doesn't automatically tie the productivity of roles in that segment to the target. You'll need a post-gen fix to route it properly. Know this before you commit to that configuration.

Tops-Down & Bottoms-Up

Tops-down sets the Not-to-Exceed guardrails — how many people Finance will fund at each level. Bottoms-up is where planners enter their actual month-by-month hiring plan against those guardrails.

Lab Debrief Answer Keys

Reference answers for the debrief questions in each configurator lab. These aren't the only valid answers — use them as a baseline and let discussion surface additional insights.

T&Q Configurator Lab — Debrief

  1. Hardest decision: Usually Data Hub Setup (ADO vs. flat file, attribute selection) or Planning Measures (adding a measure later is expensive). Push participants to articulate why it was hard — usually it's because they didn't have enough customer information. That's the lesson.
  2. Third planning measure (Services ARR): Add it now, mark it inactive if not immediately needed. Removing an unused measure is trivial; adding one post-generation requires updates across dozens of modules. The cost of adding it now is near zero.
  3. Existing V2 deployment: Check whether the existing Data Hub structure is compatible with V3. Set the "Use Existing Data Hub" flag correctly. Expect the territory file format to differ (flat file vs. per-tier). Budget extra time for data migration.
  4. Enable Quota Composition: Enable it when the customer has multiple quota types per role (e.g., Bookings Quota + New ARR Quota), or when different roles need different quota compositions. If a rep only has one quota type, you technically don't need it — but enabling it adds flexibility at low cost.
  5. Real Rule Engine rule for CloudWorks: Example — Strategic: Industry = "Technology" AND Employee Band = "5K–25K+" AND Revenue Band = "$100M+". This uses the attributes enabled in Data Hub Setup (Industry, Segment).

Segmentation Configurator Lab — Debrief

  1. Eight segments pushback: "Every extra segment multiplies complexity in T&Q and Capacity. More territory types, more coverage ratios, more planning rows. What's the actual business need for eight? Can we achieve it with four to five well-defined segments and use the Rule Engine to handle edge cases?" Push toward four or fewer.
  2. Adding a fourth scoring factor: Weights must still sum to 100%. Adding growth rate means reducing other weights proportionally. Confirm the customer has reliable growth rate data — if it's incomplete, it degrades score quality. Add it only if data quality supports it.
  3. Thirty percent missing employee count: Those accounts will have incomplete scoring. Options: (a) exclude from score-based and let them fall back to rule-based, (b) use a default/median value for missing fields, (c) use data enrichment. In production: flag this during Session Zero and don't proceed without a data remediation plan.
  4. Segments already in Salesforce: "Great — we'll import those as the starting point. The Segmentation app gives you a governed, data-driven process to review and refine those classifications every year — not just sales rep judgment. And the output feeds automatically into your territory and headcount planning."

Capacity Configurator Lab — Debrief

  1. EMEA needs different coverage ratios: This requires configuring coverage at the sub-region level — not just Americas. Going back to the Coverage & Workload page to change the planning level may require re-generation if the dimensionality is already locked. Flag this as a potential rework scenario if not caught before generation.
  2. Revenue target routing risk: The risk is generating the app with the right dimensionality but without the formula connections between the revenue target and the productivity model. Post-gen routing work takes one to two days and requires someone who understands the Capacity model structure. If not flagged early, it surfaces as a bug during Model Readiness — at the worst time.
  3. Third scenario (stretch): This is a configurator setting — the number of scenarios is set at generation time. Adding post-gen requires extending the scenario list and replicating scenario logic across modules. Estimated effort: small (half a day). Needs careful testing to confirm all pages reflect the new scenario.
  4. Ramp profile revenue impact: The simplified model (full quota from Month three) is less accurate but easier to explain. The nuanced model produces lower projected revenue in the first two months. Over a 12-month horizon for a $1M quota AE hired in January: the simplified model shows roughly $833K projected contribution; the nuanced model shows closer to $700K. The difference is whether Q1 hires are expected to contribute meaningfully. For Finance planning, this matters.

Sales Forecasting Module

Sales Forecasting is covered as a single App Overview session — no configurator, no configurator lab. The primary facilitation challenge is managing expectations: this is a newer, less configurable app. Keep the session focused on what it does well and honest about current limitations.

App Overview 45–60 min

"Sales Forecasting went generally available in March 2026 — the day after the first run of this workshop. We have it now. Let's talk about what it does, how it connects to T&Q, and what the realistic implementation conversation looks like with a customer."

"There is no configurator for this app. You get it as-is — far fewer configuration options than T&Q, Segmentation, or Capacity. That's actually fine, because the complexity here isn't in the configuration — it's in the CRM integration and in getting sellers to actually use it."

📝 Pull up the Sales Forecasting app in a demo environment if available. Walk through the homepage (quota attainment, forecast waterfall), then the pipeline analytics page. These two pages are the core of the demo — the lab guide has screenshots of both.

"The homepage shows quota attainment, forecast call, and the opportunity pipeline in one view. The key numbers: Won Attainment — what's already closed — and Commit Attainment — what's committed to close. The gap between Commit and quota is what needs to come from Upside and Pipeline."

"The pipeline analytics page is where managers live. Week-over-week pipeline change — is our pipeline growing or shrinking? Pipeline hygiene — which deals have stale stages, missing MEDDPICC fields, or close dates in the past? The app enforces CRM hygiene by making it visible. That's either going to be very popular with your Sales Ops team or very unpopular with your sales reps."

📝 Common question: "Can it write forecast decisions back to Salesforce?" Answer: Not currently — inbound data only. This is the most-asked limitation. Frame it honestly: "It's on the roadmap. Right now, Anaplan is the system of record for the forecast, not Salesforce. Some customers are fine with that. Others need bidirectionality before they'll commit."

"The critical dependency: T&Q quota data needs to be live and structured correctly before Sales Forecasting means anything. If reps don't have quotas in Anaplan, there's nothing to measure attainment against. This is why Sales Forecasting is almost always a second engagement — you implement T&Q first, get quotas right, then add Forecasting."

"One thing to prepare customers for: this app will surface their Salesforce data quality problems immediately. Stale opportunity stages, missing close dates, incomplete MEDDPICC — all of it becomes visible on day one. Brief sales leadership before go-live. Frame it as a feature, not a bug. You want to know about dirty pipeline data — that's the point."

📝 Wrap up with the roadmap context: ICM is still on the roadmap. When it ships, the full five-pillar RPM suite will be complete. Sales Forecasting going GA is a signal that the platform is maturing quickly. Keep participants engaged in the roadmap — this is a fast-moving space.
